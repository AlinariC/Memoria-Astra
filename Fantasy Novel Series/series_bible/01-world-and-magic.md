# World and Magic

## Vael Taryn

Vael Taryn is a continent built over the bodies of the first dragons. Its kingdoms look old because they are standing on older bones. Mountain chains follow the curve of buried spines. Inland seas fill collapsed wing hollows. Forests grow greener above slumbering hearts. Cities cluster where cinders warm the ground or power old machines.

The world should feel beautiful before it feels dangerous. Every region needs weather, food, work, roads, songs, fears, and a reason people stay there even when leaving would be safer.

## Major Regions

### The Crownreach

Human-ruled highlands, fortified roads, mining charters, royal foundries, tax forts, chapel towers, and soot-stained cities. The Crownreach claims civilization depends on discipline. It also depends on exploited miners and concealed cinder engines.

Key location: **Kell Ashgate**, Mara's mining city, built around vents that exhale dragon-warm air and toxic silver ash.

### The Gloamfen

A rain-soaked borderland of black reeds, fungal islands, drowned watchtowers, and floating plank roads. Spores carry fragments of memory. Goblin caravan clans know the safe paths better than any royal mapmaker.

Key location: **The Market Under the Sleeping Beast**, a goblin salvage city built around a hibernating colossal marsh creature.

### The Seven Arches

A chain of ancient bridges across a canyon so deep it shows stars at noon. Troll clans keep the arches as shrines, roads, courts, and ancestral bodies. Every crossing requires a vow, fee, duel, song, or confession.

Key location: **Third Arch**, where Mara earns Brakka's respect during a siege.

### Lumenorath

The light-elf forest-citadels, grown from white root, mirror-bark, and living coral brought inland by vanished seas. It is breathtaking, ordered, fragrant, and quietly suffocating. Beauty is law here. So is silence.

Key location: **The Pale Bough**, seat of Serathiel's saint-general order.

### Khar Veyl

The dark-elf under-realms: basalt terraces, root-fire lamps, fungal courts, echo markets, and blade schools carved into cavern walls. Khar Veyl remembers what Lumenorath refused to say aloud.

Key location: **The Banquet of True Shadows**, where every lie changes the light in the hall.

### Brassroot

Gnome guild-cities threaded through huge root systems and engine-temples. Brassroot engineers believe no mystery is sacred if understanding it could save lives. Their history proves that not every useful invention is innocent.

Key location: **The Glass Engine**, a cinder regulator that floods with liquid glass when sabotaged.

### Orrowen

The buried empire under the oldest battlefield. Bone-lit seas, dead civic districts, laws enforced by ghosts, and roads paved with dragon-scale. Orrowen is not hell. It is a civilization that refuses to admit it ended.

Key location: **The Moon Below the World**, the sealed dragon-moon beneath Orrowen that opens one eye at the end of Book Two.

### The Storm Isles

Floating rock, cloud monasteries, lightning roots, and the thermals of living dragons. To reach them, travelers ride storm currents, stolen wings, or dragon-forged lifts.

Key location: **The Court of Long Memory**, where dragons judge by witnessed memory instead of spoken testimony.

### The Dead Capital of Vael

The ruin where the first covenant was made. Each district replays a different failed ending of history: conquest, sacrifice, obedience, vengeance, and perfect stillness. The final march forces Mara to reject every easy victory the past offers.

## Cinders

Cinders are not crystals. They are the hardened memory-organs of ancient dragons, buried throughout the world after the first age. A cinder can be as small as a fist or as vast as a city foundation. It stores heat, sound, emotion, vows, pain, and old intention.

People mine cinders because they are useful:

- Warmth for cities.
- Light for roads and wards.
- Memory storage for archives.
- Power for gnome engines.
- Healing reservoirs for priests.
- Corpse animation when corrupted.
- Weapons when cracked.

The moral problem is simple: the world has been burning the dead for fuel and calling it civilization.

## Cinder-Singing

Cinder-singing is the ability to hear, interpret, and sometimes answer cinder memory. It is rare, dangerous, and politically explosive. Most cinder-singers were killed, hidden, or absorbed into priestly orders.

Mara is unusual because she hears not only the memory stored in cinders, but the dragons still dreaming through them.

## Magic Rules

1. **Memory is the medium.** Magic cannot create power from nothing. It releases, redirects, bargains with, or reshapes stored memory.
2. **Witness anchors power.** The more people who remember or witness a vow, wound, or event, the more force it can carry.
3. **Names steady the dead.** A named corpse can sometimes be spoken back into personhood. An unnamed corpse is easier to enslave.
4. **Cinders answer emotion, not commands.** Fear wakes defensive magic. Grief wakes ghosts. Rage cracks cinders. Mercy can quiet them.
5. **Every use has a cost.** Costs include ash-fever, lost personal memories, phantom pain, temporary possession by dragon memory, attracting undead, or making a buried cinder more awake.
6. **Saltwater dampens cinder resonance.** Drowned roads and inland seas matter strategically.
7. **Oaths are infrastructure.** Troll vow-law, light-elf hymns, dark-elf exile songs, goblin contracts, and undead testimonies all have real magical weight when anchored to cinders.

## Common Magic Practices

- **Cinder-warming:** domestic heat and streetlamps.
- **Vowbinding:** oath magic used by trolls and some knights.
- **Hymncraft:** light-elf order magic, beautiful and coercive.
- **Shadow-reading:** dark-elf memory espionage and concealment.
- **Gear-warding:** gnome machines that regulate cinder output.
- **Contract sparks:** goblin bargain magic sealed through tokens, teeth, ink, or shared risk.
- **Saint-calling:** undead rites that preserve testimony or bind armies.
- **Dragon witness:** the most dangerous form, where memory itself becomes evidence and weapon.

## Limits and Failure Modes

Cinders can be exhausted, poisoned, over-awakened, or cracked. A cracked cinder spills memory indiscriminately. People nearby may remember lives they never lived, obey commands meant for the dead, age unevenly, or see monsters drawn from dragon fear.

No spell should solve a problem unless it creates another problem of equal dramatic weight.
