# Series Overview

## Working Series Title

**The Cinderwake Trilogy**

## Book Titles

1. **The Ash Beneath the Crown**
2. **The Moon Below the World**
3. **The Dragon That Dreamed Death**

## One-Sentence Series Logline

An overlooked ash-runner who can hear the buried hearts of dead dragons gathers a company of enemies and outcasts to stop kings, saints, and ancient grief from turning the living world into an obedient afterlife.

## Core Dramatic Question

Can someone born powerless become powerful without becoming the kind of person who once decided her life was expendable?

## Emotional Engine

Mara Venn has survived by making herself useful to people who would never choose her freely. At the beginning, she is clever, wary, physically brave, and emotionally trained to expect dismissal. She does not believe in destiny because destiny has always been a word rich people used after the poor did the dying.

Her gift does not make her glamorous. It makes her listen. She hears grief stored in stone, old lies sealed under altars, hunger inside monsters, and the voices of dragons whose bodies were mined into power. The trilogy turns that ability into authority slowly. Mara does not win because she becomes the largest flame in the world. She wins because she learns what every faction is afraid to remember.

## The World-Level Conflict

Vael Taryn runs on **cinders**, the buried hearts and memory-organs of the first dragons. Some lie beneath mountains. Some beat faintly under drowned cities. Some have been cracked open and built into engines, altars, weapons, and throne rooms. Cinders hold memory so strongly that they can warm cities, empower wards, heal wounds, preserve ghosts, and raise the dead when abused.

For centuries, rulers have treated cinders as fuel. That economy is failing. Cinders are waking. The dead are remembering the wrong masters. Dragons thought extinct are stirring in ash, salt, root, and storm. The ancient systems that kept light elves, dark elves, gnomes, goblins, trolls, humans, undead, and dragons separated are breaking at the same time.

The first dragon, Vorrakai, does not want to burn the world. It wants to still it. Its followers believe suffering ends when choice ends. Their promised peace is a perfect, obedient afterlife laid over the living world.

## Trilogy Shape

Book One is a regional rebellion and survival quest. Mara forms the core party, discovers what cinders are, and breaks the royal foundry using her city as hidden labor for undead soldiers.

Book Two is a continental faction war. Mara becomes a symbol before she is ready, the party fractures, and the road leads beneath the world to the sealed dragon-moon that caused the ancient split between light and dark elves.

Book Three is mythic convergence. The dead march in daylight, dragons return as judges and creditors, and Mara has to reject both martyrdom and rule. The final victory is not domination. It is release, restitution, and shared stewardship.

## Reader Promise

This trilogy should deliver:

- A female underdog rising into earned leadership.
- A strong ensemble cast with combat roles, cultural conflicts, secrets, and real affection.
- Complete book arcs that resolve their central conflicts while sharpening the trilogy mystery.
- Action-rich travel through distinct environments, each changing the plot and the party.
- Magic with rules, costs, wonder, and consequences.
- Villains whose philosophies are understandable, dangerous, and wrong.
- A final ending that feels paid for, not merely big.

## IP Distance Rules

The series may use broad genre ingredients: quest travel, elves, dragons, undead, ancient ruins, fellowship dynamics, and escalating battles. It must not use recognizable settings, named concepts, class systems, races as proprietary implementations, one-object disposal plots, crystals that choose warriors of light, iconic summons, famous faction silhouettes, or a distant dark lord who only exists as shadow.

No hobbits. No borrowed maps. No borrowed cosmology. No wink-and-nudge substitutes for famous names.
