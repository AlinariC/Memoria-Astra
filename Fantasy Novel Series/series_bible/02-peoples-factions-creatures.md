# Peoples, Factions, and Creatures

## Humans

Humans dominate the Crownreach and several river kingdoms, mostly because they multiplied quickly around useful cinder sites and built institutions willing to exploit them. Human cultures should not be monolithic. Some are miners, some fisherfolk, some soldiers, some farmers, some scholars, some rebels, some collaborators.

Major human faction: **The Crown of Harrowmere**, ruled by a king whose ministers claim cinder extraction is the only thing holding back famine, cold, and border wars.

Internal conflict: survival policy versus moral rot.

## Light Elves

The light elves of Lumenorath call themselves the Elyri. They are conservators of memory, beauty, law, and sacred form. Their cities are luminous and highly ritualized. They preserve art, seed-lines, architecture, and songs that would otherwise be lost.

They also preserve lies if those lies maintain order.

Major faction: **The Pale Bough**, led in Book Two by Serathiel, a saint-general who would cleanse whole peoples if convinced it prevents another dragon catastrophe.

Internal conflict: beauty as stewardship versus beauty as control.

## Dark Elves

The dark elves of Khar Veyl call themselves the Veyra. They guard under-roads, sealed histories, and dangerous truths. Their culture prizes precision, restraint, layered speech, and the right to survive without asking the surface for permission.

They are not evil counterparts to light elves. They carry old wounds, dangerous secrets, fierce loyalties, and their own political cruelty.

Major faction: **The Umbral Seat**, a council of blade-scholars, exile judges, and memory wardens.

Internal conflict: secrecy as protection versus secrecy as poison.

## Gnomes

Gnomes are engineers, botanists, alchemists, architects, miners, field medics, and auditors of dangerous systems. Their guild-cities operate through apprenticeships, debt-ledgers, craft names, and loud public argument.

Major faction: **The Brassroot Compact**, a network of engine-temples and root-cities that regulates cinder infrastructure.

Internal conflict: invention as liberation versus invention as excuse.

## Goblins

Goblin clans are traders, scouts, salvagers, smugglers, negotiators, ruin-guides, beast-handlers, and practical historians. They were driven out of settled roads centuries ago and responded by owning the roads nobody else could survive.

Major faction: **The Kavik Road Clans**, a loose confederation of caravans, contracts, market-law, and mutual rescue debts.

Internal conflict: adaptability as freedom versus bargaining away loyalty.

## Trolls

Trolls are stone-blooded, slow-aging, physically formidable, and socially bound by place. Many clans tie identity to bridges, passes, arches, cliffs, old roads, and vow-stones. They regenerate from wounds if connected to their oath-ground, but injuries taken while oathless scar permanently.

Major faction: **The Seven Arch Clans**, keepers of the canyon bridges.

Internal conflict: vows as personhood versus vows as cages.

## Undead

The undead are not a single army. Some are enslaved corpses. Some are oathbound witnesses. Some are saints preserved by communities that still need their testimony. Some are hungry remnants. Some are bureaucrats of dead cities who continue stamping decrees in governments that no longer exist.

Major faction: **The Oathbound of Orrowen**, undead citizens who insist they are still people because they still remember their names.

Internal conflict: memory as dignity versus memory as prison.

## Dragons

Most first-age dragons are dead in body but alive in cinder memory. The living dragons are few, vast, proud, wounded, and not safe allies. Dragons think in centuries, debts, territories, weather, and witnessed truth.

Major faction: **The Court of Long Memory**, living dragons who judge disputes by presenting memories as evidence.

Internal conflict: long memory as wisdom versus long memory as tyranny.

## Major Antagonist Network

### Lord Cael Mordane

Book One antagonist. Royal cinder-minister of Harrowmere. He creates obedient undead laborers and soldiers from the poor, the imprisoned, and the unnamed dead. He believes cruelty is arithmetic: a thousand dead miners are acceptable if the kingdom survives winter.

### Serathiel of the Pale Bough

Book Two antagonist. A light-elf saint-general who appears noble, disciplined, and merciful in private moments. She believes the world must be purified before the cinders fully wake. She is wrong because she mistakes control for peace.

### Vorrakai's Choir

Book Three antagonist network. Undead kings, corrupted dragons, living rulers, and grief-sick saints who believe free will causes suffering. They do not promise destruction. They promise stillness, obedience, and an end to pain.

### Vorrakai

The first dragon, dead but dreaming through the deepest cinders. Vorrakai's grief is older than nations. It remembers every betrayal, every war, every broken covenant, and concludes that a living world cannot be trusted with choice.

## Creature Ecology

Creatures should have reasons to exist.

- **Ash wights:** miners and soldiers raised by cracked cinder dust, drawn to heat and old commands.
- **Glassback drakes:** lesser dragon-kin that nest near hot silica flats and shred metal for minerals.
- **Memory moths:** fungal lowland insects that swarm over secrets, attracted to spoken guilt.
- **Reed giants:** Gloamfen predators that mimic bell sounds to lure travelers.
- **Mirror cavalry:** undead riders visible first in reflections on salt flats.
- **Root-hounds:** gnome-bred ward beasts that can track cinder leaks.
- **Sky eels:** storm-isle predators that feed on lightning residue.
- **Archive crabs:** coral catacomb scavengers that carry scraps of writing on their shells.

Monster encounters should reveal world damage, faction choices, or the cost of magic.
