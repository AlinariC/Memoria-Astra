# Copywriter Two Pass: Market and Craft Copy

Status: alternate/reference pass. The main canon direction now uses **The Cinderwake Trilogy**, Mara Venn, cinders as buried dragon-hearts, and the book titles in `README.md`. This document remains useful for reader promise, stakes ladder, epigraph ideas, and IP-distance warnings.

Role focus: thematic consistency, reader satisfaction, and IP distance.

Working original premise assumed for this pass: An overlooked human girl from a flooded border town discovers she can hear the buried voice of a dead dragon-king through ancient stone pylons called the Resonants. Her gift makes her valuable to every faction in a continent where light elves, dark elves, gnomes, goblin courts, troll clans, undead saints, and surviving dragons all believe the old world is about to wake beneath the new one. She does not begin as royalty, a chosen knight, or a perfect prodigy. She becomes heroic because she keeps choosing people over power, even when power is the only thing that seems capable of saving them.

## Market-Facing Copy

### Alternate Series Title Candidates

1. **The Resonant Crown**
   - Strong epic-fantasy signal. Suggests ancient magic, legitimacy, and a final test of rule.

2. **The Ashen Star Trilogy**
   - High emotional charge. Carries apocalypse, celestial wonder, and rebirth.

3. **Songs of the Last Dragon**
   - Accessible and commercial. Promises dragons without sounding like a dragon-rider clone if the story keeps the dragon presence spiritual, political, and mythic.

4. **The Waking Kingdoms**
   - Best for a broad ensemble adventure. Implies multiple cultures, ancient powers, and escalating war.

5. **The Crown Beneath the World**
   - Memorable and slightly literary. Points to buried history, underworld environments, and the protagonist's rise from below.

6. **The Oathfire Cycle**
   - Good if the magic system centers on vows, cost, and sacrifice.

7. **The Shards of Vaelith**
   - Traditional fantasy shelf signal. Use only if "shards" are not magical crystals in the Final Fantasy sense.

8. **The Emberwake Trilogy**
   - Fast, clean, and marketable. Less grand than the others but easy to remember.

Recommended lead candidate: **The Resonant Crown**. It sounds epic, emotional, and original enough to build a brand around.

### Protagonist Framing

The heroine should be framed as an underdog whose power is not combat dominance at first, but perception. She hears what others have trained themselves not to hear: grief in ruins, lies in treaties, hunger inside monsters, and fear beneath command. Her market hook is not "the deadliest girl in the kingdom." It is "the girl who can listen to a dying world and refuses to let the powerful decide whose lives count."

Suggested protagonist copy:

**Mara Vey has spent her life patching nets, counting flood bells, and being told that border girls do not shape history. Then the oldest stone in the drowned town speaks her name. Now armies, dragons, elves, and the faithful dead are hunting for the one thing Mara never wanted to become: the voice that can wake a buried crown.**

Alternate names if "Mara Vey" does not fit the house style:

- **Liora Fen**
- **Sera Thorne**
- **Nyra Vale**
- **Kaelin Moss**
- **Eryn Sol**

### Emotional Promise

This trilogy should promise readers four satisfactions:

1. **Underdog ascent:** A frightened, ordinary girl earns authority through courage, loyalty, and moral clarity.

2. **Found-family devotion:** The supporting cast becomes emotionally necessary, not decorative. Readers should worry about the archer, the gnome engineer, the dark-elf exile, the undead pilgrim, and the troll oath-sister as much as they worry about the heroine.

3. **Wonder with danger inside it:** Every new environment should feel beautiful before it becomes threatening: glass deserts that sing at dawn, drowned forests lit by ghost lanterns, white elf citadels grown from living coral, black-root undercities, dragon graveyards, floating monasteries, and battlefields where the dead still pray.

4. **A hard-earned ending:** The final victory should not simply destroy evil. It should heal a broken covenant between peoples, the dead, dragons, and the land itself. The reader should close book three feeling bruised, relieved, and satisfied.

### Core Series Pitch

When the ancient Resonants begin to sing beneath the kingdoms of Vaelith, every ruler hears a different prophecy. The light elves call it the return of divine order. The dark elves call it a prison cracking open. The gnomes hear an engine restarting. The undead saints hear a debt unpaid. The dragons hear a name they buried before history began.

Only Mara Vey, a flood-town nobody with no army and no noble blood, hears the whole song.

To stop a war that is already spreading through forests, citadels, tomb roads, and sky-scarred mountains, Mara must gather a company of enemies, outcasts, and oathbreakers. But the deeper she follows the song, the clearer its warning becomes: the world was not saved in the last age. It was merely silenced. And the crown buried beneath it is not waiting for a ruler. It is waiting for a sacrifice.

### Back-Cover Direction

Book One should sell discovery, pursuit, and the first impossible victory. The ending must complete the local story: save the floodlands, expose the first betrayal, awaken one Resonant, and prove Mara is not a mistake. The hook into book two should be the revelation that awakening one Resonant has stirred the buried network.

Book Two should sell fracture, moral cost, and widening war. The ending must complete the central campaign: survive the Blackroot underworld, stop the undead host from becoming a weapon, and reunite the company after a devastating betrayal. The hook into book three should be the cost of victory: Mara has opened the way to the crown beneath the world.

Book Three should sell convergence, sacrifice, and restoration. The ending must answer the trilogy's biggest emotional question: not "Can Mara win?" but "Can Mara become powerful without becoming another ruler who decides the world from above?"

## Craft-Facing Copy

### Theme Spine

The trilogy's central theme should be:

**Power is only righteous when it remains answerable to the powerless.**

Every major faction should test that theme:

- Light elves believe order protects the weak, but their order erases dissent.
- Dark elves believe survival excuses secrecy, but secrecy corrodes trust.
- Gnomes believe invention is neutral, but machines inherit the morals of their makers.
- Undead saints believe suffering earns authority, but pain alone does not make wisdom.
- Troll clans believe vows define personhood, but vows can become cages.
- Goblin courts believe adaptability is freedom, but constant bargaining can cheapen loyalty.
- Dragons believe long memory gives them the right to judge short-lived peoples, but memory without humility becomes tyranny.

Mara should succeed because she learns from each people without becoming absorbed by any single worldview.

### Reader Satisfaction Checklist

For each book:

- Give Mara a concrete, emotionally legible goal by chapter three.
- Make the supporting cast useful in plot, not only banter.
- Let each environment change the plan, the relationships, and the stakes.
- Resolve the book's primary external conflict completely.
- Leave one larger mystery sharper at the end than it was at the beginning.
- Make every sacrifice buy something specific.
- Avoid fake-out deaths unless the resurrection or survival has a real cost.
- Let villains be wrong in ways that are understandable, not randomly cruel.
- Give the final chapter an emotional landing after the battle.

### Stakes Ladder

Book One: **Personal to regional**

- Mara wants to save her brother, her town, or the only person who believed in her.
- The floodlands are threatened by a corrupt local regent, goblin mercenary pressure, and a Resonant awakening under the marsh.
- Climactic satisfaction: Mara saves the floodlands but reveals herself to the powers of the continent.

Book Two: **Regional to continental**

- Mara's company fractures under politics, grief, and incompatible loyalties.
- The Blackroot underworld opens, giving rival factions access to undead armies and dragon-bone engines.
- Climactic satisfaction: Mara prevents the dead from being enslaved, but the buried crown begins to wake.

Book Three: **Continental to mythic**

- The living factions march toward the world-beneath-world, each claiming the right to decide the future.
- Dragons return not as simple allies but as ancient creditors.
- Climactic satisfaction: Mara refuses domination, breaks the old covenant, and forges a new one that requires shared stewardship instead of a single immortal throne.

### Chapter-Opener Epigraph Style Suggestions

Use epigraphs as worldbuilding pressure, not lore dumps. They should be short, varied, and occasionally unreliable.

Possible recurring epigraph sources:

- **Floodbell records** from Mara's town: practical, local, increasingly tragic.
- **Light-elf hymns**: beautiful, formal, chilling when read beside their politics.
- **Dark-elf exile songs**: intimate, coded, full of double meaning.
- **Gnome field manuals**: funny at first, then frightening as inventions become weapons.
- **Troll vow-stones**: blunt, poetic, honor-centered.
- **Goblin contract fragments**: witty, sharp, morally flexible.
- **Undead saint testimonies**: serene and unsettling.
- **Dragon memory tablets**: vast, arrogant, and grief-stricken.

Sample epigraph tones:

> The bell is not rung for the dead. The dead know where they are. It is rung for the living, who must be reminded.
> - Floodbell Record, Year of Long Rain

> Never call a machine innocent. Innocence is not a property of tools. It is a confession made by cowards.
> - Marginal note in a gnome artificer's manual

> A vow is a bridge. Cross it often enough, and it becomes a road. Break it once, and both shores remember.
> - Troll vow-stone, western pass

> We did not bury the crown because it was evil. We buried it because it obeyed.
> - Fragment attributed to the last dragon-king

### IP Distance Warnings

Avoid making the central magic "crystals that choose warriors of light." That combination points too strongly toward Final Fantasy. If crystals exist, make them mundane, geological, or culturally specific rather than the core cosmic system.

Avoid a fellowship built around carrying one corrupting object into a volcanic dark land. That silhouette is too close to The Lord of the Rings. This trilogy can have a company, a journey, mountains, ancient evil, and temptation, but the plot engine should be awakening, covenant, and political-mythic repair rather than object disposal.

Avoid "light elves good, dark elves evil." That is generic and often tired, but it can also drift toward existing fantasy settings. Make both societies morally complicated and internally diverse.

Avoid naming anything too near established fantasy brands: no Midgar, Balamb, Eorzea, Hydaelyn, Zodiark, Lifestream, Materia, Azeroth, Stormwind, Undercity, Scourge, Mordor, Gondor, Rivendell, Mirkwood, Valinor, Silmaril, Drow, Drizzt, Dragonlance, Krynn, Westeros, Essos, White Walkers, or One Ring equivalents.

Avoid a dark lord who is only a distant eye, shadow, or disembodied will. If there is a central antagonist, give them social presence, a persuasive philosophy, and direct relationships with the cast.

Avoid dragon-rider academy framing unless the story intentionally wants that market lane. Dragons here are stronger as gods, witnesses, debts, and living catastrophes than as mounts.

Avoid MMO class labels in the prose. A warrior, mage, rogue, healer, tank-style party can be useful underneath the craft, but the reader should meet them as people with cultures and wounds, not as a balanced raid composition.

### Copywriter Two Recommendation

The best commercial angle is not "Final Fantasy meets Lord of the Rings" on the page. That is useful as a development compass, but the public-facing promise should be:

**A sweeping, emotional epic about an ordinary girl who hears a buried world waking and gathers enemies into a family before kings, dragons, and the dead tear the living world apart.**

That gives the market wonder, scale, action, and heart without leaning on comparison titles so hard that the trilogy feels derivative.
